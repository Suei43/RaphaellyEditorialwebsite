Investment in Research: The Road to Development

The role of research in society and its place in human welfare improvement cannot be over-emphasized. Indeed, various civilizations through the ages thus far evolved from man's involvement in research. Although research is the effort of man's rigorous inquiry and articulation of a process that reveals why, how, when, what and where of a thing or process, its impact is seen in the application of findings and documentation of fresh knowledge in addition to what already exists. In modern times, the research process has become more robust with the availability of quality technology, which itself is the product of rigorous research.
Research is largely responsible for the rate of development and level of comfort in countries irrespective of whether they are rich or poor. Evidently, the gap between rich and poor countries or between developing and developed economies is the result of investment in research. Rich countries continue to invest huge sums of money yearly in research and consequently, apply findings and knowledge from such research to aspects of life and society where they are needed. To the contrary, developing and most poor countries tend in practice to accord less significance to investment in research. These countries actually acknowledge the importance of research in all facets of life, but the meager sums of money budgeted for research are indications that research is not receiving top priority in their development agenda.
An examination of the educational facilities and budget set aside for education in developing countries will show that most developing nations are far behind the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization's (UNESCO) recommendation of budgetary allocation to education. Education remains the bedrock of research and should be given adequate attention in the world's current changing environment. The budget of most developing countries is far less than what the United States and Japan alone devote to research and development yearly. It is therefore no surprise that Africans who can afford it rush to developed countries for further studies.
Evidence abounds to show that universities in developed countries are best equipped for research and collaboration. This status is reflected in the yearly ranking of universities in the world. Reports of such ranking have it that universities in America, Europe and Asia have always notched up the top positions. Investment in research and contribution to both knowledge and society are part of the major criteria for determining the ranking of universities. And these among others should determine the relevance of any higher institution. Practically every area of human life receives input from research. Do we mention life expectancy, health care or methodologies for improvement in learning? Knowledge in these areas has been deepened and extended by means of research.
In the same vein, equipment and other resource materials have continued to receive research attention in the developed countries. The main objective is to improve efficiency. For developing African countries to improve social welfare standards and measure up with developed countries, their political leaders and administrators of the national system should begin to give more significant priority to research. One way of doing this is to increase the budget allocation to research in universities and research institutions. Public and private institutions could also seek support from international organizations. The way out of poverty, ignorance and under-development is to invest in education, embrace research, disseminate research findings and apply them.
